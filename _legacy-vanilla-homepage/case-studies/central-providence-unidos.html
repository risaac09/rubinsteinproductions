Central Providence
Unidos.

Shared measurement and evaluation framework for a Rhode Island Health Equity Zone collaborative. 60+ partner organizations. Three-layer design — RBA, Water of Systems Change, racial equity screen. Bilingual.

client
One Neighborhood Builders (backbone)
role
Associate Director of Evaluation and Learning
period
Jul 2024 — Dec 2025
geography
Rhode Island

Engagement summary.

One Neighborhood Builders is the backbone organization for Central Providence Unidos — a Rhode Island Health Equity Zone, place-based collective impact initiative funded under the state Department of Health's HEZ designation, with additional support from a federal CDC Foundation cooperative agreement and several national and regional foundations. The initiative coordinates 60+ partner organizations and a stipended Resident Advisory Council across five strategy pillars: housing, health, education, quality work, and civic power.

When the engagement began, the collaborative had strong programmatic coordination but no unified evaluation framework. Partners tracked outputs inconsistently, funders required different reporting formats, and there was no shared definition of what population-level progress would look like across pillars. The engagement was scoped to design and stand up a shared measurement system, embed evaluation into the FY25 action plan, and build the data infrastructure that would let the collaborative report to multiple funders without duplicating effort.

Evaluation questions.

  1. How much is the collaborative doing across each strategy pillar?
  2. How well is it being done — quality, fidelity, partner satisfaction with backbone supports?
  3. Is anyone better off — at the participant level and at the population level?
  4. Are systems-level changes shifting in ways consistent with the collaborative's theory of change?
  5. Is the collaborative advancing racial equity in measurable ways across material conditions, process, and decision-making power?
  6. Is the backbone organization providing supports that partner organizations rate as effective, and where are the gaps?

Three-layer framework.

Layer 1 — Results-Based Accountability

Operational backbone for partner-level and program-level reporting. Every strategy was structured around the three RBA questions (How much? How well? Is anyone better off?), giving partners and funders a shared reporting grammar.

Layer 2 — Water of Systems Change

Indicators specified at three depths: structural changes (policies, practices, resource flows), relational changes (relationships and connections, power dynamics), and transformative changes (mental models, dominant narratives). This let the collaborative report progress on systems shifts that would not appear in standard output metrics. (Kania, Kramer, Senge, 2018.)

Layer 3 — Racial equity screen

Each evaluation question and indicator was screened against three criteria: does it address root causes of racial inequity, does it advance solutions emerging from the experience of communities of color, and does it improve material conditions. The screen was applied during indicator selection, not retrofitted after the fact.

Indicator framework.

The published dashboard tracks 14 population-level "North Star" indicators across six health equity domains, plus a separate set of process and partner-level indicators. Each indicator has a documented definition, source, and notes on representativeness and bias (e.g., self-report sampling error in ACS data, undercounting in eviction filings due to informal evictions, lead-testing coverage gaps, exclusion of out-of-state ED visits). Bilingual definitions in English and Spanish are published alongside the dashboard so community users can interpret each indicator without intermediation.

  • Housing — cost burden, eviction filings, low/moderate income inventory
  • Health — insurance coverage, pediatric lead poisoning prevalence, asthma-related ED visits
  • Education — multilingual learner four-year graduation rate, bilingual educator certifications, family-rated favorability of special education
  • Quality work — business ownership by race and gender, employment-to-population ratio, resident-rated job opportunity
  • Power — juvenile arrest rate, voter turnout, descriptive representation ratio
  • Climate — Tree Equity Score (composite)

Stakeholder engagement.

Engagement was designed into the evaluation at five points: indicator co-selection by pillar working groups; Resident Advisory Council interpretation sessions; the annual Collaborative Member Survey; a bi-annual RAC experience survey; and recurring bilingual Data and Evaluation Learning Hours.

Protocols emphasized the distinction between consultation (input that may or may not be acted on) and co-decision (input that demonstrably moves the work). The distinction was made explicit at the start of each session.

Findings.

  1. Shared measurement reduced funder reporting friction and partner reporting burden. Multi-funder reporting was producible from a single data layer.
  2. The systems-change layer captured progress that RBA alone would have missed. Several of the most consequential FY25 outcomes — policy shifts, new institutional partnerships, changes in how city and state agencies engaged with resident input — registered at structural and relational depths but would not have moved an output metric.
  3. Embedding the racial equity screen at indicator-selection stage changed which questions got asked. Indicators that did not advance solutions emerging from communities of color were dropped or rebuilt. The screen affected what counted as data, not just how data was presented.
  4. Resident Advisory Council interpretation produced data the dashboard alone did not surface. Patterns in lead exposure, eviction filings, and bilingual education capacity that RAC members named from lived experience preceded — and in two cases predicted — movement in the underlying quantitative indicators.
  5. Evaluation utilization tracked closely with how participatory the framework was. Partners and residents who were involved in indicator selection used the dashboard. Partners who received the dashboard as a finished product used it less.

Artifacts produced.

  • Published Tableau dashboard — 14 North Star indicators, six domains, bilingual
  • FY25 Evaluation Plan, released to the state Department of Health and partner funders
  • FY25 Action Plan matrix integrating evaluation metrics with strategy commitments
  • Annual Collaborative Member Survey (bilingual instrument and report)
  • Bi-annual Resident Advisory Council experience survey (bilingual)
  • Indicator definition library with sources, bias notes, update cadence (English + Spanish)
  • Year-in-Review report (FY25)
  • Data and Evaluation Learning Hour curriculum and bilingual facilitation materials

Letters of reference, dashboard demonstration, FY25 Evaluation Plan, and full deliverable samples available on request.
