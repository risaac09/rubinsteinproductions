Engagement summary.

Seattle Children's Hospital retained the consultant team to lead change management readiness, hypercare support, and sustainment measurement for the Phase 2 rollout of the Workday enterprise resource planning (ERP) and human capital management (HCM) platform. The engagement spans pre-go-live readiness assessment, go-live hypercare, and post-go-live sustainment of training assets, business processes, and stakeholder feedback loops.

Embedded within the change-management scope is a utilization-focused evaluation function: the pulse-check methodology, attendance and adoption tracking, FAQ resolution analysis, and Mock Close survey instrumentation are designed to produce decision-driving evidence that informs sustainment planning, training redesign, and post-go-live support allocation. The evaluation work is not a separate audit; it is integrated into the operating rhythm of the change-management program and is reported to executive sponsors on a recurring cadence.

Evaluation questions.

  1. To what extent are end users prepared to perform their Phase 2 responsibilities at go-live, by functional area and role?
  2. What categories of friction (process, system, training, communication) generate the most frequent questions in hypercare, and how does the distribution shift across hypercare iterations?
  3. What is the participation rate, attendance pattern, and engagement quality of the Change Champion Network across the hypercare period?
  4. Which job aids and process maps are being adopted in practice, which are being bypassed, and where are workarounds emerging?
  5. After the Mock Close exercise, how confident do back-office staff feel about the live Month End Close, where do they identify the highest residual friction, and what additional support do they request?
  6. Which hypercare interventions correlate with reduced question volume and improved sustainment indicators?

Framework and design.

Utilization-focused evaluation embedded within an organizational change-management program. The design is formative-developmental: data collection iterations inform the next iteration of training, communication, and support design rather than producing a single summative report.

  • Readiness assessment layer — pre-go-live measurement of preparedness by functional area, surfacing gaps for targeted remediation before go-live.
  • Hypercare measurement layer — repeated pulse checks, FAQ tracking, and attendance monitoring across the hypercare period, producing a rolling picture of adoption velocity and friction concentration.
  • Sustainment measurement layer — Hypercare Sustainment Tracker (versioned through three iterations) records the stabilization of business processes, the lifecycle of job aids, and the transition of support burden from implementation team to operational owners.
  • Stakeholder feedback layer — Mock Close survey and structured feedback channels (Change Champion Network, office hours) capture user experience and inform the close of the readiness cycle.

Stakeholder engagement.

The Change Champion Network is a network of designated functional-area representatives who attend pulse-check sessions, surface questions on behalf of their teams, and serve as the primary channel for cascading hypercare communication. Pulse-check sessions are facilitated as structured Q&A: questions are logged, attributed to the asker, routed to the correct answerer, and tracked through to resolution in the Pulse Check Question Tracker (134 questions tracked to date).

The participatory orientation is operational, not rhetorical. Artifact ownership, FAQ authorship, and feedback channels are distributed across the affected functional areas rather than concentrated in the change-management team.

Functional working groups (Finance, Sponsored Research, Payroll Costing, Requisitioner, ILT) are convened around domain-specific FAQ and process design questions. On-site demo sessions are recorded, transcribed, and converted into structured spreadsheet additions — the transcript-to-spreadsheet methodology preserves operational detail that would be lost in summary minutes.

Interim findings.

  1. Concentrated friction in sponsored research and federal draw workflows. A disproportionate share of FAQ volume clusters in committed cost share, federal draw netting, and invoice adjustment versus credit memo handling. Operational design decisions are surfacing late in the readiness cycle, suggesting upstream design-phase stakeholder engagement gaps that should be addressed before any future ERP phase rollout.
  2. Job aid adoption tracks more closely with sustainment-phase reinforcement than with go-live training. ERP training-resource investment should be weighted toward the sustainment period rather than concentrated pre-go-live.
  3. Change-management interventions and technical fixes are reliably distinguishable when triaged early. The early triage decision (CM intervention vs. technical fix) has held up across the engagement, suggesting the theme-log triage protocol is reusable for future ERP implementations as a screening tool.
  4. Mock Close exercises produce confidence shifts that correlate with specific friction points. Open-ended responses identify recurring friction at the same handful of process steps even where Likert ratings are moderately positive — summary preparedness scores alone would obscure the specific support needs.
  5. The pulse-check FAQ tracker is functioning as a real-time training needs assessment. Domain distribution of questions provides a leading indicator of where supplementary training and office-hours capacity should be allocated.

Transferable learnings.

  • Embedding evaluation within a hypercare operating rhythm (rather than running it parallel) produces faster decision cycles and higher utilization, but requires that the evaluator be a credentialed, accountable participant in the change-management program.
  • Structured pulse-check protocols with public question-tracking outperform open-comment surveys for surfacing actionable friction, particularly in technically complex environments.
  • Triage of qualitative themes into "change-management intervention" vs. "technical fix" prevents misallocation of CM resources to issues that require system reconfiguration.
